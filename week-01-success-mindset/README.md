# Assignment 1. What is something you believe to be true that most people around you would disagree with?

### Rules

* No "safe" answers.
* Must be your real belief (not copied from internet).
* Minimum 50 words.

**Hint:** What do you believe about career, money, learning, discipline, relationships, health, success, life, tech industry, etc. that most people don't agree with?

## Answer

One belief I have that many people around me don't agree with is that your degree or your current job does not determine the direction of your career, your willingness to keep learning does.

Many people believe that once you start working in a particular field, you should remain there for the rest of your career because changing paths is too risky or unrealistic. I used to hear people say that technology is only for those who studied Computer Science or started programming at a young age.

My experience has shown me something different. While working in payments operations, I became curious about cloud computing and DevOps. Instead of waiting for the "perfect opportunity," I started learning after work, building projects, earning certifications, and documenting my progress. It hasn't been easy balancing work and study, but every project I complete proves that consistent effort creates opportunities that qualifications alone cannot.

I believe people underestimate what disciplined learning over a long period can achieve. Skills can be learned, experience can be built, and careers can change when someone commits to continuous improvement instead of waiting for ideal circumstances.

# From Spreadsheets to Server Infrastructure: How One Nigerian Banking Professional Rebuilt His Career in Five Years

Five years ago, he spent his days reconciling transactions, reviewing settlement reports, and ensuring financial records were accurate at a bank in Lagos, Nigeria. Today, he designs and maintains cloud infrastructure, automates deployment pipelines, and collaborates with distributed engineering teams across different time zones. The industries changed, the responsibilities evolved, but the mindset that made the transition possible remained the same: consistent learning and disciplined execution.

His career shift did not begin with a resignation letter or a breakthrough opportunity. It began with a simple decision to invest in himself. While working full-time in banking operations, he dedicated his evenings and weekends to learning Linux, Git, Docker, Terraform, Jenkins, Kubernetes, AWS, and modern DevOps practices. Progress was slow at first, but every small project added another layer of confidence and experience.

One of the defining moments in that journey came when he joined the DevOps Micro Internship (DMI) Cohort 3. The program gave his learning structure, accountability, and exposure to real-world DevOps practices. Rather than stopping at tutorials, he focused on building practical solutions. He deployed containerized applications with Docker, automated CI/CD workflows, provisioned cloud infrastructure using Terraform, and documented every project with clear explanations and architecture diagrams.

Over time, his GitHub portfolio became more than a collection of repositories. It became evidence of steady growth. Each project demonstrated not only technical knowledge but also his ability to troubleshoot problems, document solutions, and continuously improve his work. Recruiters and hiring managers could clearly see the progression from beginner projects to production-oriented infrastructure automation.

As his experience grew, he earned industry-recognized cloud certifications that strengthened the practical skills he had developed through hands-on work. He also began sharing his journey through LinkedIn posts and technical articles, explaining concepts in a way that was practical and accessible to beginners. His willingness to document both successes and challenges helped many aspiring engineers, especially professionals transitioning from non-technical backgrounds, believe that career change was possible with persistence and consistent effort.

Within a few years, he secured a remote DevOps engineering role with an international technology company. There, he contributed to infrastructure automation, improved deployment pipelines, enhanced system reliability, and collaborated closely with software engineers to build scalable cloud solutions. His reputation was built not only on technical competence but also on his reliability, communication skills, and calm approach to solving complex problems.

Beyond his day-to-day responsibilities, he remained committed to giving back to the community that supported his growth. He mentored aspiring cloud engineers, participated in technical communities, contributed to open-source projects and documentation, and regularly shared practical learning resources. His goal extended beyond advancing his own career; he wanted to shorten the learning curve for others beginning a similar journey.

Looking back, his biggest achievement was not changing careers, it was changing the way he approached learning. The systems, discipline, and consistency he developed during that transition became the foundation for every success that followed. His journey proved that career growth is rarely defined by where someone starts, but by what they choose to build every single day.

# Assignment 4. Have you ever cut corners (unethical / dishonest / shortcut behavior — not necessarily illegal)? If yes, how did it make you feel?

### Important

You don't need to write the full story.

Focus on the feeling:

* guilt
* fear
* shame
* stress
* regret
* numbness
* etc.

This is about self-awareness, not judgment.

### Answer Format

**Yes / No**

If Yes:

**What emotion did you feel?** (minimum 50–100 words)

## Answer

#Yes.

There have been times when I looked for shortcuts instead of fully understanding what I was learning. For example, while working on technical projects, I occasionally copied commands or solutions from documentation or online forums without taking enough time to understand why they worked. Although it helped me finish a task faster, I later realized that I struggled to explain or troubleshoot the same problem on my own.

The feeling was not so much guilt as it was disappointment in myself. I knew I had completed the task, but I also knew I hadn't truly learned from it. That experience taught me that finishing quickly is not the same as growing. Since then, I have made a conscious effort to slow down when necessary, read documentation carefully, experiment with different approaches, and write notes in my own words. The progress feels slower at times, but I now retain more knowledge and have much more confidence when solving similar problems independently.

# Reflection – Week 1

### Biggest insight I got about myself this week

The biggest insight I gained this week was that long-term success is built on systems, not good intentions. During the DMI Cohort 3 onboarding, I realized this program is not just about learning DevOps tools—it is about developing the discipline, consistency, and execution mindset required to succeed in the tech industry.

As someone balancing a full-time role in banking operations while transitioning into Cloud and DevOps, I know my biggest challenge is not a lack of interest but staying consistent over several months. The onboarding helped me understand that making small, steady progress every week is far more valuable than trying to do everything at once. It also reinforced the importance of documenting my work, building real projects, managing my time intentionally, and taking ownership of my learning. My biggest takeaway is that if I trust the process and remain consistent, I will be in a much stronger position five months from now than I am today.

### My biggest weakness/loop I noticed

One pattern I have noticed is that I sometimes spend too much time trying to make my work perfect before I consider it ready. Whether it's a GitHub project, documentation, a LinkedIn post, or an assignment, I often keep revising because I feel there is always something that could be improved. While paying attention to detail is important, I have realized that this habit can slow my progress and reduce the amount of work I actually complete.

The DMI onboarding reminded me that consistent execution is more valuable than perfection. Going forward, I want to focus on completing tasks to a high standard, learning from feedback, and improving through iteration instead of waiting for everything to feel perfect before moving on.

### One system I will implement from this week (exact habit + time)

Starting this week, I will dedicate 8:45 PM to 10:30 PM every Monday to Thursday as my non-negotiable DevOps deep work block. During this time, I will focus only on learning, building projects, or completing my DMI assignment. My phone will be on Do Not Disturb, and I will avoid social media until the session is complete.

On Sunday from 4:00 PM to 7:00 PM, I will begin working on the week's DMI assignment immediately after the Saturday live session. This will help me make steady progress throughout the week instead of rushing to complete everything on Friday before the submission deadline. At the end of each study session, I will spend 10–15 minutes documenting what I learned and updating my GitHub or personal notes.
